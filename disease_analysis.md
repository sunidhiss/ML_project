### Disease-wise Analysis: Vector-Borne Diseases (Dengue and Malaria)

Dengue and malaria are climate-sensitive vector-borne diseases whose transmission is strongly influenced by rainfall and seasonal weather patterns. The monsoon rainfall analysis (Figure: Monsoon Rainfall Pattern) shows that a significant proportion of annual rainfall in India occurs between June and September, with peak precipitation in July and August.

Heavy monsoon rainfall leads to the accumulation of stagnant water in open drains, containers, construction sites, and low-lying areas. These conditions create ideal breeding environments for mosquitoes, particularly Aedes and Anopheles species, which are responsible for transmitting dengue and malaria respectively. Additionally, increased humidity during the monsoon enhances mosquito survival and biting activity.

The year-wise rainfall trend further indicates inter-annual variability in precipitation levels. Years with above-average rainfall often correspond to increased risk of vector-borne disease outbreaks, as prolonged wet conditions extend the mosquito breeding season. Post-monsoon periods are especially critical, as residual water bodies continue to support mosquito populations even after peak rainfall subsides.

Thus, the combined seasonal concentration and year-wise variability of rainfall play a crucial role in determining the timing and intensity of dengue and malaria outbreaks across India.


### Disease-wise Analysis: Water-Borne Diseases (Cholera and Acute Diarrheal Disease)

Water-borne diseases such as cholera and acute diarrheal disease are closely associated with rainfall intensity and water quality. The monsoon season, characterized by heavy and sustained rainfall, often leads to flooding and the overflow of drainage and sewage systems, particularly in regions with inadequate sanitation infrastructure.

Excess rainfall can contaminate surface and groundwater sources by mixing sewage with drinking water supplies. This significantly increases the risk of cholera and diarrheal disease transmission, especially in densely populated areas where access to clean water is limited. Unlike vector-borne diseases, water-borne diseases often show rapid outbreaks immediately following intense rainfall events.

The seasonal concentration of rainfall during the monsoon months therefore plays a critical role in triggering water-borne disease outbreaks. Regions experiencing frequent flooding or prolonged rainfall are particularly vulnerable, highlighting the importance of climate-aware public health planning and water management strategies.


### State and District-Level Vulnerability Insights

Rainfall-based analysis indicates that regions experiencing consistently high monsoonal precipitation are more vulnerable to climate-sensitive disease outbreaks. Sub-divisions receiving sustained rainfall during the monsoon months are exposed to increased risks of both vector-borne and water-borne diseases.

High population density further amplifies this vulnerability by facilitating faster disease transmission, particularly in urban and semi-urban districts. Inadequate sanitation infrastructure, poor drainage systems, and limited access to clean drinking water exacerbate the impact of climatic stress in these regions.

Districts located in flood-prone or coastal areas are especially susceptible, as excessive rainfall often leads to prolonged water stagnation and contamination. These combined climatic and socio-demographic factors make certain regions persistent high-risk zones, emphasizing the need for targeted surveillance, early warning systems, and climate-informed public health interventions.

In densely populated regions, even moderate climatic stress can result in disproportionately large outbreaks due to close human contact and overburdened public health infrastructure.
